Important Information

Why Girona

Catalan medieval gem on the way to the Pyrenees

Girona is the kind of city that travel writers describe as "perfectly preserved" — except here it's true. Roman walls, a medieval Jewish quarter that produced the great Kabbalist Nahmanides, a Gothic cathedral with the widest nave in Christendom, painted houses reflecting in the Onyar river, and an iron footbridge designed by Gustave Eiffel before he built his famous tower. All within an old town you can walk end-to-end in 20 minutes.

Day Itinerary — 6 Stops

Thursday, August 6 — Full Day in Girona

From Girona station, cross the River Onyar on foot — the Cathedral towers are visible from the bridge.

Stop 1 · 9:15 AM

Rambla de la Llibertat & the Onyar River Houses

Free

As you cross the Onyar from the station, pause on any of the bridges for the quintessential Girona photograph — the row of tall, pastel-painted houses (ochre, terracotta, mustard, salmon) stacked vertiginously along the riverbank. These houses were built directly onto the old medieval city walls in the 16th–17th centuries. The colours weren't decorative — they helped fishermen identify their own homes from the river.

The most famous of the bridges is the Pont de les Peixateries Velles (Eiffel Bridge) — a bright red iron footbridge designed by Gustave Eiffel in 1877, before he built his famous tower in Paris. Same engineer, same signature latticed ironwork, a fraction of the fame.

Cross over and walk up the Rambla de la Llibertat — a shaded arcade of medieval market buildings, now home to cafés, florists, and the weekly market. This is where Girona has its morning coffee.

Stop 2 · 9:45 AM

El Call — The Medieval Jewish Quarter

Free to walk

Turn off the Rambla onto Carrer de la Força — the oldest street in Girona, following the path of the original Roman road — and you enter El Call, one of the best-preserved medieval Jewish quarters in Europe.

The first Jewish families arrived in 890 AD, invited by the local lord. By the 13th century the community had grown into a major centre of Kabbalistic scholarship — the great rabbi Nahmanides (Moses ben Nachman, 1194–1270) was born here, and his teachings on the mystical interpretation of the Torah spread across the Jewish world.

Walk the impossibly narrow lanes — Carrer de Sant Llorenç, Carrer Manuel Cundaro — where the stone walls lean toward each other overhead, blocking out the sky. In 1391 a violent pogrom killed dozens of Girona's Jews; by 1492 the entire community was expelled under the Edict of Expulsion. The last synagogue was sold to the Church just before the deadline. For nearly 500 years the quarter was erased from memory — only restored and celebrated since the 1990s.

Stop 3 · 10:30 AM

Museu d'Història dels Jueus

~€4ppCarrer de la Força 8Mon–Sat 10am–6pm · Sun 10am–3pm

The Museum of Jewish History occupies the building of Girona's last synagogue — sold to the Church in 1492 and now returned to memory.

Highlights: the Star of David mosaic in the central courtyard, directly above the site of the last synagogue altar; the mikveh (ritual bath) discovered beneath the building, dating to the 11th century; a collection of Hebrew tombstones considered one of the most important in Spain; and the original Edict of Expulsion. The Nahmanides Institute of Jewish Studies is housed here. Allow 45–60 minutes.

Stop 4 · 11:45 AM

Girona Cathedral & the 90 Steps

~€7ppcatedraldegirona.cat

From El Call, Carrer de la Força leads directly up to the Cathedral's monumental baroque staircase — 90 steps (it feels like more in August heat). Roman temple, then Visigothic church, then mosque after the Islamic conquest in 717 AD, then reconsecrated by Charlemagne's Franks in 785 AD.

Built across four centuries (11th–18th): a Romanesque cloister and bell tower (12th c.), a Gothic nave (14th–15th c., the widest in the world at 23 metres — wider than Notre Dame, Reims, or Chartres), and a Baroque façade (begun 1606, finished in the 1960s).

Inside don't miss: the Tapestry of Creation (11th/12th century — one of the finest Romanesque textiles in the world, depicting Genesis), the gem-studded silver altarpiece (14th c.), and the Chair of Charlemagne. The staircase featured in Game of Thrones Season 6 as Cersei's Walk of Shame — you'll recognise it immediately.

Stop 5 · 1:00 PM

The Medieval City Walls (Passeig de la Muralla)

FreeAccess near Cathedral or Torre Gironella

After the Cathedral, find the steps up to the Passeig de la Muralla — the walkway along Girona's ancient city walls. First built by the Romans, expanded by Charlemagne's Franks, reinforced medievally, survived the Napoleonic siege of 1808–1809.

The walkway stretches ~2km along the eastern rim of the old city, passing towers, gatehouses, and hidden gardens. Panoramic views west over terracotta rooftops, east toward the green foothills of the Pre-Pyrenees, north along the Onyar valley. On a clear August day the distant Pyrenean peaks are visible. 30 minutes covers the best sections. Free, no ticket needed.

Getting Around Girona

Walking Only
Station → Old Town
Cross the River Onyar on any of the colourful painted bridges. The Jewish Quarter, Cathedral, and medieval walls are all within 800m of the station. No bus or taxi needed.
Old Town
Entire old town walkable in 20 minutes end-to-end. Follow the river toward the Cathedral.
If Needed
Taxis outside Girona station €5–8 to anywhere in the old town. But truly — walking is the only way to experience Girona properly.
